Quantum Currency Workshop · Step 0 · Field · Zero Point

Before we count anything,
let's find the ground.

Quantum field theory tells us that the field is more fundamental than the particle. What emerges from a system depends entirely on the conditions of the field it emerges from.

"The field is not between things. The field is primary. Things are what the field does locally."

In quantum mechanics, zero point energy is the irreducible minimum that remains even when everything else has been stripped away. It is not emptiness. It is the ground that is never truly empty — the energy that persists before anything else begins.

The Sower lens — a diagnostic we carry through every step

The path

Seed lands but never roots. Information exists; it doesn't travel.

Rocky ground

Fast enthusiasm, no depth. Grows quickly, burns on friction.

Thorny ground

Too many competing demands. Energy leaks in all directions.

Good soil

Coherent, self-sustaining. The seed multiplies — ten-fold, hundred-fold.

What soil have you been planting in — and what soil are you standing in right now?

This question is not rhetorical. We are going to answer it together.

Why this step exists

Step 0 is not a warm-up. It is the most important thing in the workshop. The ground state of the room determines everything that follows. If anyone in the room is defended, distracted, or performing competence rather than actually present — no framework will root. The Sower lens is the tool. Presence is the soil. This step exists to create the conditions before anything else can grow.

How the session opens

The session begins before the slide. Something true gets named — not a credential, not a summary of what's coming. A moment of genuine recognition: a time when best work met thorny ground, or something took root in good soil unexpectedly. The field set in the first five minutes is the field the whole workshop grows in. A slide signals a presentation. A story signals a conversation. One of these rooms is more honest than the other.

Reading the room — soil signals

Path signals

Phones out within the first five minutes. Questions that are really defences ("we already do something like this"). The CEO answering for the team before they can speak.

Rocky ground signals

High energy, fast agreement, lots of "yes, exactly!" with no specifics offered. Enthusiasm that doesn't slow down to examine anything. The room that loved your last session but implemented nothing.

Thorny ground signals

People who keep bringing in other priorities mid-exercise. "That's interesting but our situation is different because—" The CEO who keeps expanding scope rather than focusing it.

Good soil signals

Genuine pauses before answering. Someone naming a hard truth without being asked to. The CEO who looks at their team with curiosity rather than authority. Silence that feels productive.

When the soil needs tending — what shifts things

Path room: Everything slows down. One direct, specific question to one person — then silence held. The seed needs somewhere to land before anything continues.
Rocky room: An example gets named. "Can you tell me about a specific time that happened?" Depth is the pivot. Nothing moves forward until at least one person has gone below the surface.
Thorny room: The pattern gets named gently. "I notice we keep expanding. For the next ten minutes, let's hold just this one question." Constraint is the pivot. The discernment step (3) may need to arrive sooner than planned.
Good soil room: Trust it and get out of the way. The question gets asked — then listening. The room does the work. The urge to over-facilitate is worth noticing and setting down.

What this step reveals

How a leader responds to the Sower lens in Step 0 is data. A leader who immediately asks "how do I make my team good soil?" without first sitting with the question themselves is telling you something. A leader who pauses, looks at the four soils, and says quietly "I think we've been thorny ground for a while" — that is a room worth building in. This step is not just teaching. It is finding out if this is a field worth sowing.

The personal soil audit

Think back across your professional relationships — collaborations, partnerships, employers, clients, teams. Place at least one in each quadrant. Name the relationship however feels right (initials, a role, a project name). Then note briefly: what made that soil what it was?

The path

The seed never rooted. What happened to the information or energy you brought?

Rocky ground

Enthusiasm that didn't hold. What was missing underneath?

Thorny ground

Good work, crowded out. What were the competing forces?

Good soil

Something took root and multiplied. What made this ground different?

2

The present moment

Without judgment — where does this business feel like it is right now? You can place it in more than one quadrant if that feels honest. Note what evidence you're drawing on.

3

The field you arrived with today

What did you bring into this room? What are you carrying that might make it harder to be present — a worry, a pressure, a prior conclusion about what the answer should be? Name it briefly. Naming it is how you set it down.

4

The zero point question

If you stripped away every role, every deliverable, every metric — what is the energy this business is actually operating from? Not what it produces. What it generates from within.

To carry forward — the Sower question

At every step of this workshop, we will return to one question: what kind of ground are we in right now?

Not as a judgment. As a calibration. The seed — the framework, the model, the strategy — doesn't change. What changes is how honestly we can see the ground we're planting it in.

The hundred-fold yield is not a miracle. It is what coherent systems do when the conditions are right. Our work together is to build those conditions — starting here, in this room, in this conversation, right now.
